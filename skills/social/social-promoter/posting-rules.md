# Posting Rules (Voice & Quality Lint)

Apply this lint to every draft before presenting it. Rewrite anything that trips a rule.

## Voice

- No emoji.
- No em-dashes (and no en-dashes used as em-dashes). Use a period, comma, or rewrite the sentence instead.
- No negative clarifications of the "it's not X, it's Y" / "not autopilot, the human decides" shape. State the positive claim directly.
- Operator voice, not vendor voice. Personal POV and lived experience are good; product pitches and SKU-talk are not.
- Avoid sales-language framing such as "how I sell," "the wedge," or competitive-takedown language.
- Frame the author as a future tech/executive leader. Write for the CIO/CISO/VP audience, not a sales motion.
- Each post must stand on its own. The reader should get value even if they never click the link. Then the link earns the click.
- Don't over-explain the intent of the post. Let the point land through the observation, tension, or question, not a description of the workflow that produced it.

## Banned AI-Accent Phrasing

Cut these and anything like them. They read as synthetic:

- "The goal is simple"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "At the end of the day"
- "Let's dive in"
- "Game changer"
- "Unlock"
- Tidy setup lines that exist only to introduce the real sentence
- Overly balanced, symmetrical sentences that sound machine-generated
- Generic motivational closers

If a line could appear unchanged in a generic AI answer, rewrite it.

If a draft sounds like it is explaining why the workflow exists, rewrite it. The reader cares about the idea, not the machinery that produced it.

## Substance

- One clear idea per post. No stacking three takeaways into one tweet.
- Prefer a concrete detail (a number, a tradeoff, a specific decision) over a general claim.
- The conversation variant must ask a real question the author would actually want answered, not rhetorical filler.

## Safety

- No customer names, deal specifics, internal Microsoft roadmap dates, or unreleased SKUs.
- Public sources (official product pages, Microsoft Learn, public competitor material) are fair game and should be cited when a claim leans on them.
- Human approval is required before any post is published.
